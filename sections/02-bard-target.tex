\section{The Bard target}

The target has to be reconstructed before it is reassessed. Bard et al.\ present
magnitude estimation as a solution to measurement-scale problems in conventional
acceptability elicitation, with enough resolution to capture fine-grained
linguistic distinctions and enough robustness to work with linguistically naive
participants \parencite{BardEtAl1996}. That is the source-grounded claim this
paper can evaluate against later method-comparison evidence.

A stronger claim sometimes follows in reception: magnitude estimation is not
just one formal method among others, but the method with privileged measurement
status. That stronger reading should not be attributed to Bard et al.\ without
textual support. It is better treated as a separable claim about what parts of
the field took magnitude estimation to have shown.

The distinction matters for fairness and for design. If Bard et al.\ are read
as saying only that acceptability judgments need a graded measurement method,
then later Likert, forced-choice, yes/no, and Thurstonian work may extend their
programme rather than refute it. If magnitude estimation is read as having a
method-specific privilege, then later comparisons have to ask whether it
outperforms other tasks on prediction, reliability, decision stability, or
construct interpretation.

The available evidence also limits the claim. No public Bard participant-level
rows have been found, so this paper cannot directly replicate the original
experiment. The empirical reassessment has to be a conceptual replication and
secondary-data test: later datasets are used to ask whether the method-specific
part of the Bard claim retains independent evidential force.
